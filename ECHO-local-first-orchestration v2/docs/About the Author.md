About the Author
Why ECHO Could Only Have Been Built by Someone Who Has Seen Real‑World Failure
Michael Burger is a systems‑builder, mixed‑media artist, and lifelong craftsman whose work spans glassblowing, fabrication, welding, homelab engineering, and cognitive‑architecture design. His background is unconventional for the AI world — and that is precisely why ECHO exists.
Before working with local‑first AI systems, Michael spent years in environments where failure has consequences. Not theoretical consequences. Real ones.
He has seen machines fail.
He has seen people hurt.
He has worked in spaces where one missed step, one skipped permit, or one overlooked safety line could cost a life.
Those experiences shaped his understanding of systems, risk, and responsibility. They taught him that:
safety must be structural, not optional
boundaries must be enforced, not assumed
tools must be predictable, not hopeful
workflows must be inspectable, not magical
humans must remain in control, always
This perspective is woven into every layer of ECHO.
Where many AI systems are built from abstraction, optimization, or corporate incentives, ECHO is built from craft, risk awareness, and a deep respect for the fragility of human life and human attention.
Michael approaches AI the same way he approaches a hot shop, a welding rig, or a confined‑space rescue:
with clarity, humility, and an uncompromising commitment to safety.
ECHO is not a product of Silicon Valley.
It is a product of lived experience — of someone who has seen what happens when systems fail and people pay the price.
That is why ECHO is:
constitutional
modular
local‑first
non‑surveillant
deterministic
human‑paced
refusal‑capable
safety‑anchored
It is not designed to replace human judgment.
It is designed to protect it.
Michael lives in South Carolina with his family, where he continues to build tools, art, and systems that help people work with more dignity, clarity, and control.
